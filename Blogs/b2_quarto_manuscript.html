Why Write Papers in RStudio?

Most researchers today already use R and RStudio for their data analysis. We run models, generate tables, and produce figures, all within the same environment.

But when it comes to writing the actual manuscript, we often switch tools: exporting results to Word, formatting references in citation managers, or handing over LaTeX code to journals. This adds friction, duplication, and errors.

The question is: why leave RStudio when you can write your manuscript there too?

With Quarto, you can now write your entire paper in RStudio, combining:

  • Your text and manuscript structure.
  • Your code chunks (R, Python, Julia, etc).
  • Figures and tables, perfectly placed and automatically updated.
  • References and citations handled automatically.
  • Multiple output formats (Word, PDF, HTML) in one click.

It‚Äôs a single, reproducible workflow: from data to manuscript.

Adding References

Quarto uses a references.bib file (BibTeX format). Example entry:

@article{smith2020,
  title={Data Science in Healthcare},
  author={Smith, John and Doe, Jane},
  journal={Journal of Health Analytics},
  year={2020},
  volume={12},
  number={3},
  pages={123-135}
}

In your manuscript, cite it like this:

Research in this field is expanding rapidly [@smith2020].

At the end, Quarto automatically generates the bibliography.

Want APA style? Add this to the YAML:

csl: apa.csl

(Download styles from Zotero‚Äôs style repository).

Rendering Your Paper

In RStudio, click the Render button (or run quarto render in the terminal).

This generates:

  • Word document (index.docx) ‚Äî ready to share with supervisors or collaborators.
  • PDF (index.pdf) ‚Äî beautifully typeset.
  • HTML (index.html) ‚Äî great for online publishing or lab websites.

All from the same source file.
