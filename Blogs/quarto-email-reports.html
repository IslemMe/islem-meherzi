A Quick and Safe Way to Share Quarto Reports with Restricted Users

Quarto
R
Automation
Automate sending Quarto reports to a restricted audience via email using R, Gmail App Passwords, and the blastula package, with a ready-to-use script and alternatives like Netlify and GitHub Actions.
Author

Islem Meherzi

Published

October 7, 2025

Introduction

Sometimes you need to share your updated reports with a restricted group of people instead of publishing them openly. A practical and secure way is to automate sending your reports by email. With Quarto and R, you can render your reports, attach them as PDF or HTML, and deliver them directly to your recipients’ inboxes.

In this post, I will show you how to achieve this with R and Quarto, using Gmail as an example.

Step 1. Generate a Gmail App Password

To allow R to send emails from your Gmail account, you must create a Gmail App Password. This is safer than using your main password.

  1. Enable 2-Step Verification in your Google Account Security settings.
  2. Go to the App Passwords page.
  3. Choose the app and device (e.g., Mail → Other → “R script”).
  4. Click Generate. Google will display a 16-character password.
  5. Copy and save this password immediately, as it cannot be viewed again later.

This password is what you will use in your R script instead of your usual Gmail password.

Step 2. Ready-to-Use R Script

Below is a complete R script that renders your Quarto report (report.qmd), produces PDF and HTML versions, and sends them as email attachments. You only need to replace the email addresses and the Gmail App Password.

# --- Load packages ---
library(quarto)
library(blastula)

# --- Step 1: Render your Quarto report into PDF and HTML ---
quarto_render("report.qmd", output_format = "pdf")
quarto_render("report.qmd", output_format = "html")

# --- Step 2: Create the email body ---
email <- compose_email(
  body = md("
  Hello,  

  The updated Quarto report is ready.  
  Please find both PDF and HTML versions attached.  

  Best regards,  
  Islem
  ")
)

# --- Step 3: Attach the reports ---
email <- add_attachment(email, file = "report.pdf")
email <- add_attachment(email, file = "report.html")

# --- Step 4: Send the email ---
smtp_send(
  email,
  from = "your_email@gmail.com",
  to = c("recipient1@example.com", "recipient2@example.com"),
  subject = paste0("Updated Report - ", Sys.Date()),
  credentials = creds(
    host = "smtp.gmail.com",
    port = 587,
    user = "your_email@gmail.com",
    use_ssl = TRUE
  )
)
# The app password will be requested interactively in a popup after running, no need to hard-code it

Step 3. Automate the Process

To make this fully automated:

  • On Windows, schedule the script with taskscheduleR.
  • On Linux/macOS, use cronR or add it to your system cron jobs.
  • Every time the script runs, your Quarto report will be rendered and sent to the selected users automatically.

Other Options

Email automation is not the only way to share Quarto reports with restricted users:

  • Netlify with password protection: host your Quarto site and restrict access with a password.
  • GitHub Actions or GitLab CI: automate report rendering and emailing or publishing.
  • Posit Connect: enterprise solution that provides user access control, scheduling, and automatic email notifications out of the box.

Choose the approach that best fits your context and your audience’s needs.

Conclusion

For many small teams or individuals, automating email delivery is a quick, safe, and effective way to share Quarto reports with a limited group of users. By combining Quarto, R, and the blastula package, you can render, attach, and distribute updated reports with minimal effort.
